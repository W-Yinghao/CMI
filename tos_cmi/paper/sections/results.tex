\section{Results --- EEG measurement-to-control}

\subsection{Setup (one paragraph)}
We freeze a trained backbone per LOSO fold and dump its penultimate latent $Z$ on the source subjects (the
held-out target subject is used for reporting only, never in selection, gating, or probing). On the frozen
$Z$ we (i) localize a candidate domain-rich, task-light subspace $V_D$ with the score-Fisher generalized
eigenproblem (\S2.2), (ii) delete it by the $M$-oblique direct-sum projector (\S2.3) and measure subject and
task decodability on the complement $RZ=(I-P_N)Z$ with linear and MLP probes, against a same-$k$ random-removal
control, and (iii) sweep a global LPC penalty (CE $+ \lambda \cdot I(Z;D|Y)$) to compare selective deletion with the
standard global objective. We study two representations: TSMNet (LogEig/SPD tangent latent, $d_z=210$) and
EEGNet (convolutional penultimate latent, $d_z=16$).

\subsection{TSMNet: subject leakage is high-dimensional and redundant; global LPC ``works'' only by collapse}
\textbf{In the high-dimensional TSMNet latent, the framework localizes subject-preferential low-rank directions,
but deleting them does not remove subject leakage.} Subject identity is almost perfectly decodable from
the full $Z$ (MLP 0.997, chance 0.125; Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}A); the score-Fisher selector localizes a compact candidate
subspace (nDcand $\approx$ 3 of 210; Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}D), yet deleting $V_D$ only \textbf{dents} subject decode (0.997$\to$0.96 MLP) ---
essentially matching same-$k$ random removal ($\approx$0.997; Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}A,C) --- while preserving the task (0.75$\to$0.75;
Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}B). Even deleting the full Fisher-discriminative subspace (the LDA cap of 7 directions for 8 source
subjects) leaves subject decode at 0.92--0.98: subject identity is \textbf{redundantly re-encoded} across the
high-dimensional latent, so \textbf{low-rank selective deletion is insufficient} \claimtag{C4}.

\textbf{Control is negative (global penalty).} The global LPC penalty \emph{appears} to remove leakage at high $\lambda$, but
the per-epoch curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}A--C) show this is a sharp, $\lambda$-tied \textbf{collapse of the representation to the
origin}: task cross-entropy rises to $\ln 4$ (chance), the feature norm and top singular value go to 0, and
the penalty itself goes to 0 (trivially satisfied by $Z\to0$). This is an objective-scaling pathology, not a
gradient explosion (the diagnostic encoder gradient is $\sim$10$\times$ smaller than in healthy low-$\lambda$ training and
never non-finite) and not a smooth geometric over-compression (the ``effective rank stays high'' is a
scale-invariant-metric artifact) \claimtag{C5}. The collapse is \emph{fixable} by a warm-up schedule (or, at $\lambda=1$, a
scale-normalized penalty), confirming its optimization origin --- but every collapse-free, task-preserving
variant leaves subject decode at the ERM level (Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}D), i.e. \textbf{once the collapse is prevented, the global
penalty removes no leakage} \claimtag{C6}. In the tested TSMNet/2a setting, global LPC's apparent de-domaining is a
collapse artifact.

\subsection{EEGNet: low-rank deletion linearly reduces leakage, but removal yields no generalization benefit}
\textbf{EEGNet shows that the negative TSMNet result is not because score-Fisher deletion can never remove
leakage: in a compact latent the same diagnostic deletion removes much more subject information --- yet this
removal does not translate into target-domain improvement.} Deleting $V_D$ (nDcand $\approx$ 5 of 16) preserves the
task (0.64$\to$0.64) and \textbf{selectively removes} subject decode far below same-$k$ random removal: linear
0.82$\to$0.35 (random 0.73), MLP 0.88$\to$0.54 (random 0.81; Fig.~\ref{fig:eegnet}A--C). The informed-vs-random selectivity is
$\sim$0.35--0.55, roughly an order of magnitude larger than on TSMNet (0.04--0.08), and same-$k$ random deletion
does \emph{not} reproduce it --- genuine subspace selectivity, not an artifact of deleting a larger fraction of a
small latent \claimtag{C7}. The removal is \textbf{partial}: a substantial nonlinear residual survives (RZ MLP 0.54, well
above chance 0.125), so we report it as \emph{linearly reducible with a persistent nonlinear residual}, not as
elimination \claimtag{C8}.

\textbf{But removable $\ne$ beneficial.} Sweeping the global LPC penalty on EEGNet reduces subject decode
monotonically (0.89$\to$0.17) \emph{without} collapse (the feature norm never goes to 0; Fig.~\ref{fig:eegnet}D), yet \textbf{mean} LOSO
target accuracy is flat-to-worse (0.43$\to$0.39 across $\lambda$; paired-t worse at every $\lambda$, p$\le$0.001 at $\lambda\ge$1) and
uncorrelated with leakage reduction (Pearson $-$0.14, n.s.). Removing the measured leakage --- by selective
deletion or by a collapse-free global penalty --- does \textbf{not} improve cross-subject generalization on
EEGNet/2a \claimtag{C9}. \emph{(We report the mean LOSO accuracy, the standard DG metric; the median is $\sim$flat and would
obscure the paired decline.)}

\subsection{Stronger linear erasure does not close the control gap}
\textbf{The measurement-to-control gap is not an artifact of a weak eraser: a stronger, closed-form optimal
linear eraser removes more subject information than the TOS deletion, yet the gap remains} (Table~\ref{tab:erasure}).
On both backbones LEACE --- the closed-form optimal linear eraser --- drives \emph{linearly} decodable
subject identity to chance ($0.12$) and reduces subject decodability more than the TOS $V_D$ deletion at
comparable task cost (MLP residual TSMNet $0.96\to0.74$, EEGNet $0.55\to0.39$); the rank-limited erasers
(RLACE, TOS) by contrast fail on the redundant high-dimensional TSMNet latent ($0.86$--$0.96$) and only work
on the compact EEGNet latent. So the score-Fisher \emph{projection is not} the methodological advantage [C7].
The stronger eraser instead sharpens the phenomenon: after \emph{optimal} linear erasure a large
\textbf{nonlinearly recoverable subject residual} remains under held-out MLP probes on TSMNet ($0.74\gg$
chance $0.125$) versus a smaller one on EEGNet ($0.39$) --- consistent with nonlinear/redundant subject
coding that linear erasure cannot reach. INLP can drive subject decodability to chance only by destroying
task information (task $0.75\to0.55$ TSMNet, $0.64\to0.25$ EEGNet): unconstrained erasure is not task-safe.
Crucially, none of these erasers --- including the optimal linear one --- converts subject-leakage removal
into a target-accuracy gain (the global-LPC sweep of \S4.3 makes the same point along a continuum). Successful
erasure is therefore not the right endpoint; the relevant question is whether the intervention is task-safe
\emph{and} beneficial for source-to-target generalization [C9, C10].

\subsection{Unified interpretation}
Across both representations, conditional domain leakage is a \textbf{measurable} property of the EEG latent that
the score-Fisher diagnostics localize, but it is \textbf{not a sufficient control target} for cross-subject
generalization (Table~\ref{tab:summary}). Removing it can (i) collapse the representation (TSMNet global LPC), (ii) leave
redundant high-dimensional leakage essentially intact (TSMNet low-rank deletion), or (iii) genuinely
remove leakage yet not improve transfer (EEGNet). Whether the leakage even \emph{sits} in a low-rank removable
subspace is representation-dependent and, in our two-backbone design, \textbf{capacity-mediated}: a high-capacity
210-d latent redundantly re-encodes subject identity outside any low-rank subspace, while a compact 16-d
latent cannot. The correct operating principle is therefore a \textbf{certified intervention with refusal}: the
framework should delete only when a source-risk certificate permits, and abstain otherwise --- which on
TSMNet means abstain (deletion insufficient) and on EEGNet means a diagnostic deletion that removes leakage
but is not claimed to improve generalization \claimtag{C10}.

\textbf{In sum,} on these representations conditional domain leakage is measurable and sometimes (partially) removable, yet neither its measurement nor its removal demonstrates a cross-subject generalization benefit.

\emph{Limitation flagged here (full text in \S6.3): representation type and latent dimensionality are collinear in
this design (SPD$\leftrightarrow$210, conv$\leftrightarrow$16); this study establishes representation dependence, not its causal factor.}
