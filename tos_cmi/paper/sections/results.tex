\section{Results --- EEG measurement-to-control}

\subsection{Setup (one paragraph)}
We freeze a trained backbone per LOSO fold and dump its penultimate latent $Z$ on the source subjects (the
held-out target subject is used for reporting only, never in selection, gating, or probing). On the frozen
$Z$ we (i) localize a candidate domain-rich, task-light subspace $V_D$ with the score-Fisher generalized
eigenproblem (\S2.2), (ii) apply the $M$-metric deletion diagnostic used in these EEG experiments (\S2.3; the certified
task-protected direct-sum variant is part of the framework but is not exercised here) and measure subject and
task decodability on the complement $RZ=(I-P_N)Z$ with linear and MLP probes, against a same-$k$ random-removal
control, and (iii) sweep a global LPC penalty (CE $+ \lambda \cdot I(Z;D|Y)$) to compare selective deletion with the
standard global objective. We study two representations: TSMNet (LogEig/SPD tangent latent, $d_z=210$) and
EEGNet (convolutional penultimate latent, $d_z=16$).

\subsection{TSMNet: subject leakage is high-dimensional and redundant; global LPC ``works'' only by collapse}
\textbf{In the high-dimensional TSMNet latent, the framework localizes subject-preferential low-rank directions,
but deleting them does not remove subject leakage.} Subject identity is almost perfectly decodable from
the full $Z$ (MLP 0.997, chance 0.125; Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}A); the score-Fisher selector localizes a compact candidate
subspace (nDcand $\approx$ 3 of 210; Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}D), yet deleting $V_D$ only \textbf{dents} subject decode (0.997$\to$0.96 MLP) ---
essentially matching same-$k$ random removal ($\approx$0.997; Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}A,C) --- while preserving the task (0.75$\to$0.75;
Fig.~\ref{fig:tsmnet}B). Even deleting the full Fisher-discriminative subspace (the LDA cap of 7 directions for 8 source
subjects) leaves subject decode at 0.92--0.98: subject identity is \textbf{redundantly re-encoded} across the
high-dimensional latent, so \textbf{low-rank selective deletion is insufficient} \claimtag{C4}.

\textbf{Control is negative (global penalty).} The global LPC penalty \emph{appears} to remove leakage at high $\lambda$, but
the per-epoch curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}A--C) show this is a sharp, $\lambda$-tied \textbf{collapse of the representation to the
origin}: task cross-entropy rises to $\ln 4$ (chance), the feature norm and top singular value go to 0, and
the penalty itself goes to 0 (trivially satisfied by $Z\to0$). This is an objective-scaling pathology, not a
gradient explosion (the diagnostic encoder gradient is $\sim$10$\times$ smaller than in healthy low-$\lambda$ training and
never non-finite) and not a smooth geometric over-compression (the ``effective rank stays high'' is a
scale-invariant-metric artifact) \claimtag{C5}. The collapse is \emph{fixable} by a warm-up schedule (or, at $\lambda=1$, a
scale-normalized penalty), confirming its optimization origin --- but every collapse-free, task-preserving
variant leaves subject decode at the ERM level (Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}D), i.e. \textbf{once the collapse is prevented, the global
penalty removes no leakage} \claimtag{C6}. In the tested TSMNet/2a setting, global LPC's apparent de-domaining is a
collapse artifact.

\subsection{EEGNet: low-rank deletion linearly reduces leakage, yet the global-LPC sweep shows no DG gain}
\textbf{EEGNet shows that the negative TSMNet result is not because score-Fisher deletion can never remove
leakage: in a compact latent the same diagnostic deletion removes much more subject information at no task cost ---
yet, as the global-LPC sweep below shows, reducing this leakage does not translate into target-domain improvement.} Deleting $V_D$ (nDcand $\approx$ 5 of 16) preserves the
task (0.64$\to$0.64) and \textbf{selectively removes} subject decode far below same-$k$ random removal: linear
0.82$\to$0.35 (random 0.73), MLP 0.88$\to$0.54 (random 0.81; Fig.~\ref{fig:eegnet}A--C). The informed-vs-random selectivity is
$\sim$0.35--0.55, roughly an order of magnitude larger than on TSMNet (0.04--0.08), and same-$k$ random deletion
does \emph{not} reproduce it --- genuine subspace selectivity, not an artifact of deleting a larger fraction of a
small latent \claimtag{C7}. The removal is \textbf{partial}: a substantial nonlinear residual survives (RZ MLP 0.54, well
above chance 0.125), so we report it as \emph{linearly reducible with a persistent nonlinear residual}, not as
elimination \claimtag{C8}.

\textbf{But removable $\ne$ beneficial.} Sweeping the global LPC penalty on EEGNet reduces subject decode
monotonically (0.89$\to$0.17) \emph{without} collapse (the feature norm never goes to 0; Fig.~\ref{fig:eegnet}D), yet \textbf{mean} LOSO
target accuracy is flat-to-worse (0.43$\to$0.39 across $\lambda$; paired-t worse at every $\lambda$, p$\le$0.001 at $\lambda\ge$1) and
uncorrelated with the amount of leakage reduced (Pearson $-$0.14, n.s.). Reducing the measured leakage via the (collapse-free) global penalty does \textbf{not} improve
cross-subject generalization on EEGNet/2a; and when the frozen \emph{selective} erasers are deployed on the
target (\S4.5, Table~\ref{tab:target_deploy}), none improves target accuracy either \claimtag{C9}. \emph{(We report the mean LOSO accuracy, the standard DG metric; the median is $\sim$flat and would
obscure the paired decline.)}

\subsection{Stronger linear erasure does not close the control gap}
\textbf{The measurement-to-control gap is not an artifact of a weak eraser: a stronger, closed-form optimal
linear eraser removes more subject information than the TOS deletion, yet the gap remains} (Table~\ref{tab:erasure}).
On both backbones LEACE --- the closed-form optimal linear eraser --- drives \emph{linearly} decodable
subject identity to chance ($0.12$) and reduces subject decodability more than the TOS $V_D$ deletion at
comparable task cost (MLP residual TSMNet $0.96\to0.74$, EEGNet $0.55\to0.39$); the rank-limited erasers
(RLACE, TOS) by contrast fail on the redundant high-dimensional TSMNet latent ($0.86$--$0.96$) and only work
on the compact EEGNet latent. So the score-Fisher \emph{projection is not} the methodological advantage~\claimtag{C7}.
The stronger eraser instead sharpens the phenomenon: after \emph{optimal} linear erasure a large
\textbf{nonlinearly recoverable subject residual} remains under held-out MLP probes on TSMNet ($0.74\gg$
chance $0.125$) versus a smaller one on EEGNet ($0.39$) --- consistent with nonlinear/redundant subject
coding that linear erasure cannot reach. INLP drives \emph{linear} subject decodability near chance (a nonlinear residual persists on TSMNet) only by sacrificing
task information (task $0.75\to0.55$ TSMNet, $0.64\to0.25$ EEGNet): unconstrained erasure is not task-safe.
Crucially, these controls establish that the source-side removal result is not limited by a weak eraser; whether
source-side removal improves \emph{target} accuracy is a separate question, which the global-LPC sweep (\S4.3)
and a direct frozen-erasure target-deployment test (\S4.5, Table~\ref{tab:target_deploy}) both answer in the
negative. Successful
erasure is therefore not the right endpoint; the relevant question is whether the intervention is task-safe
\emph{and} beneficial for source-to-target generalization~\claimtag{C9,C10}.

\subsection{Deploying the erasers on the target: no accuracy gain}
\textbf{Deployed on the held-out target, no eraser improves target accuracy --- closing the loop the
source-side controls (Table~\ref{tab:erasure}) leave open.} For each backbone, seed, and LOSO fold we fit each
eraser on the source only, apply it to source and target, train a linear task head on the transformed source,
and evaluate on the transformed held-out target; the target is never used for fitting, selection, or
calibration (Table~\ref{tab:target_deploy}; paired fold-cluster $95\%$ CIs over $3$ seeds $\times\,9$ folds).
This is a frozen-representation linear-head deployment assay, so absolute bAcc is not comparable to the
backbone/LPC classifier in Fig.~\ref{fig:eegnet}; the statistic of interest is the paired $\Delta$ against the
full-$Z$ frozen baseline under the same head. Across both backbones, \emph{no eraser improves target balanced accuracy}: LEACE, RLACE, and TOS-$V_D$ have
$\Delta$bAcc CIs that overlap or fall below zero, and INLP collapses the task to chance (target bAcc
$0.33$/$0.25$; source task destroyed, $0.75\to0.53$ / $0.64\to0.25$). The \emph{only} positive movement is a
small NLL reduction, and it is \textbf{not subject-erasure-specific}: same-$k$ \emph{random} removal produces a
same-sign reduction (TSMNet $\Delta$NLL $-0.034$ vs LEACE $-0.031$; on EEGNet both are small) while leaving
source subject decode essentially intact ($0.998$), and INLP's larger NLL drop coincides with task collapse ---
a generic dimensionality/regularization effect, not a domain-removal benefit \claimtag{C12}. This
is the deployment evidence the preceding sections deferred: even an \emph{optimal} linear eraser, applied to a
frozen target, does not convert leakage removal into a cross-subject accuracy gain.

\subsection{Removability is dominated by latent dimension, with a residual architecture effect at high $d_z$}
\textbf{A three-seed architecture$\times$latent-dimension factorial shows that latent \emph{dimension} is the
dominant axis of the TSMNet/EEGNet removability contrast, but does \emph{not} fully account for it: a residual
architecture effect persists at high dimension.} We vary the latent dimension of each backbone with the
protocol, dataset, and probes held fixed (TSMNet tangent dimension via the SPD size, $d_z\in\{21,36,55,105,210\}$;
EEGNet penultimate width, $d_z\in\{16,32,64,128,210\}$) and measure the nonlinear (MLP) subject-decode residual
after optimal linear erasure over $3$ seeds $\times\,9$ LOSO folds, with fold-cluster $95\%$ CIs
(Fig.~\ref{fig:capacity}). The residual rises monotonically with $d_z$ \emph{within both architectures}
(OLS $\log d_z$ coefficient $+0.089$, CI $[0.086,0.092]$) and the two \emph{coincide} at low dimension
(matched-dim contrasts at $d_z\!\approx\!16$--$21$ and $32$--$36$ straddle zero: $+0.004\,[{-}0.008,0.014]$ and
$-0.008\,[{-}0.022,0.004]$). Matching dimension removes about two-thirds of the original
TSMNet-$0.74$-vs-EEGNet-$0.39$ gap, so capacity is the primary driver. \emph{However}, the effect is not purely
dimensional: TSMNet's residual grows faster with $d_z$ (interaction $+0.058\,[0.051,0.063]$), and at matched
$d_z{=}210$ the SPD/LogEig latent retains significantly more recoverable subject identity than the
convolutional one ($+0.111\,[0.094,0.125]$). We therefore report the contrast as \textbf{largely
capacity-mediated with a residual architecture$\times$dimension interaction at high capacity}, not as a pure
dimension effect (\S6.3) \claimtag{C11}.

\subsection{Multi-dataset deployment validation: erasure still does not become a DG lever}
\textbf{The target-deployment conclusion is not specific to 2a: across four additional real EEG datasets, no
principled source-fitted eraser yields a practically meaningful target-accuracy gain in any valid
dataset--backbone cell} (Table~\ref{tab:bigN_compact}; full per-eraser table with CIs in
App.~\ref{tab:bigN_full}). C12 is confirmed on all $9$ valid cells (2a, 2b-EEGNet, and Lee/Cho/High-Gamma both
backbones); 2b-TSMNet is excluded as a degenerate $3$-channel SPD configuration, not a negative cell. The
decisive additions are the \emph{non-degenerate} big-$N$ TSMNet cells: LEACE $\Delta$bAcc is $-0.001$
(Cho2017, $52$ subjects), $-0.002$ (Lee2019, $54$), and $-0.001$ (High-Gamma), all with $95\%$ CIs inside
$[-0.006,+0.003]$ --- so the TSMNet result is not a $9$-subject / $22$-channel artifact. \emph{Task-safety is heterogeneous, however}: on the binary datasets (Lee, Cho) with the compact
EEGNet latent, LEACE and RLACE drive the source task to chance ($0.79\to0.50$, $0.77\to0.50$), so at deployment
they \textbf{actively harm} the target ($\Delta$bAcc $-0.15$ to $-0.19$) --- a task-safety failure, not useful
invariance. This makes the certified-intervention-with-refusal framing more, not less, necessary: an always-on
eraser would degrade these datasets \claimtag{C13}.

\subsection{Unified interpretation}
Across both representations, conditional domain leakage is a \textbf{measurable} property of the EEG latent that
the score-Fisher diagnostics localize, but it is \textbf{not a sufficient control target} for cross-subject
generalization (Table~\ref{tab:summary}). Removing it can (i) collapse the representation (TSMNet global LPC), (ii) leave
redundant high-dimensional leakage essentially intact (TSMNet low-rank deletion), or (iii) genuinely
remove leakage yet not improve transfer (EEGNet). Whether the leakage even \emph{sits} in a low-rank removable
subspace appears \textbf{largely capacity-mediated} (a 3-seed factorial, \S4.5): a higher-capacity latent
redundantly re-encodes subject identity outside any low-rank subspace, while a compact latent cannot ---
though a residual architecture effect remains at high $d_z$. The correct operating principle is therefore a \textbf{certified intervention with refusal}: the
framework should delete only when a source-risk certificate permits, and abstain otherwise --- which on
TSMNet means abstain (deletion insufficient) and on EEGNet means a diagnostic deletion that removes leakage
but is not claimed to improve generalization \claimtag{C10}.

\textbf{In sum,} on these representations conditional domain leakage is measurable and sometimes (partially) removable, yet neither its measurement nor its removal demonstrates a cross-subject generalization benefit.

\emph{Limitation flagged here (full text in \S6.3): the two backbones differ in both architecture and latent
dimension; the capacity factorial (\S4.5; 3 seeds) shows the contrast is largely --- but not fully ---
explained by latent dimension, with a residual architecture effect at high $d_z$.}
